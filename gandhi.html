Mahatma Gandhi: Freedom Fighter and Controversial Global Icon

A balanced exploration of Gandhi’s achievements, philosophy, and historical debates surrounding his legacy.

1869–1948 Non-Violence Independence Leader Historical Debate
By Historical Research Team • Published 2025 • 18 min read

Early Life and Background

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi was born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, India. He studied law in London and later worked in South Africa, where he first experienced racial discrimination.

These experiences shaped his philosophy of non-violence and truth, known as Satyagraha, which later became the foundation of India’s independence movement.

Role in India’s Independence Movement

Gandhi returned to India in 1915 and became a leader of the Indian National Congress. He led several major movements against British rule.

  • Non-Cooperation Movement (1920) – Boycott of British goods and institutions
  • Salt March (1930) – Protest against British salt tax
  • Quit India Movement (1942) – Demand for complete independence

These movements mobilized millions of Indians through non-violent resistance.

Philosophy of Non-Violence (Ahimsa)

Gandhi’s philosophy was based on Ahimsa (non-violence) and Satyagraha (truth-force).

Non-violence is strength, not weakness

Requires courage and discipline

Truth must guide action

Means must be as pure as the goal

Peaceful resistance can change society

Mass movements without violence

Criticisms and Controversies

1. Partition and Political Decisions

Some historians argue Gandhi’s approach during India’s partition did not prevent large-scale communal violence.

2. Views on Caste System

Gandhi opposed untouchability but was criticized by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar for not fully dismantling the caste structure.

3. Economic Ideas

His focus on rural economy and spinning wheel (khadi) was seen by some as impractical for modern industrial development.

4. Gender and Social Views

Some scholars critique his views on gender roles and personal experiments related to celibacy.

Legacy and Global Influence

Gandhi’s philosophy of non-violence influenced global leaders such as Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela. His ideas remain central to discussions on peaceful resistance and civil rights movements.

Today, Gandhi is remembered as both a national hero and a complex historical figure whose legacy continues to be studied and debated.
